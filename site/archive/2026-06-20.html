The Daily Brief

The Queen, the Oxhide, and the Sea: The Rise of Carthage as a Mediterranean Power

---

Every empire begins somewhere. For Carthage, it began with a trick, a tragedy, and a woman who refused to be governed by her grief.

---

The Narrative Hook

Picture the coast of North Africa sometime around 814 BCE — though scholars will argue about that date, as they argue about nearly everything concerning this city. The sea is the color of hammered bronze in the late afternoon. A fleet of Phoenician ships, their hulls painted with the deep red-purple dye that made their homeland famous, has dropped anchor in a sheltered bay. On the shore, a local Berber chieftain named Iarbas — or Hiarbas, depending on which ancient author you trust — stands with his arms crossed, suspicious and unimpressed.

The woman negotiating with him is named Elissa. She is a princess of Tyre, one of the great trading cities of the Levantine coast, and she has arrived here with a convoy of refugees, treasure, and a story she would rather not tell in full. She wants land. Iarbas is willing to sell her only as much as an oxhide can cover.

Elissa orders the oxhide cut into the thinnest possible strips. Then she has those strips laid end to end in a great circle around the hill that will become known as the Byrsa — a word that means, with exquisite irony, "oxhide." On that hill, she founds a city. The Greeks will call it Karchedon. The Romans will call it Carthago. We call it Carthage.

This story is almost certainly a legend, polished to a gleam by centuries of retelling. But it contains a truth that purely factual accounts often miss: Carthage was built on cleverness, and cleverness — commercial, diplomatic, and military — would define it for the next six centuries.

---

The World Before

To understand what Carthage became, you first have to understand what the Mediterranean world looked like before it rose to dominance.

In the ninth century BCE, the sea was already old with commerce. Egyptian merchants had been sailing it for a thousand years. Mycenaean Greeks had traded and raided its coasts before their civilization collapsed in the mysterious catastrophe of around 1200 BCE, an event so total that it wiped out palace economies, disrupted trade routes, and sent the whole eastern Mediterranean into a dark age that lasted for generations. By the time the world was recovering — by the time Elissa's ships were crossing the water — the Phoenician cities of the Levantine coast had stepped into the vacuum.

The Phoenicians were not empire-builders in the conventional sense. They did not march armies across continents or impose tribute on conquered peoples. They were traders, and extraordinary ones. From their home cities of Tyre, Sidon, and Byblos, they spread outward along the Mediterranean coast like ink dropped in water, establishing not colonies in the Greek sense but trading posts — what the Romans would call emporia — at strategic points where raw materials could be gathered and goods exchanged. They reached Cyprus, Sardinia, Sicily, and Spain. They almost certainly sailed beyond the Pillars of Hercules — what we now call the Strait of Gibraltar — into the Atlantic. One ancient account, disputed but tantalizing, claims a Phoenician fleet circumnavigated Africa sometime in the seventh century BCE.

Tyre, the mother city of Carthage, was the jewel of this network. It sat on an island just off the coast of modern Lebanon, virtually impregnable, and it controlled the production of Tyrian purple dye — extracted from sea snails in a process so laborious and so foul-smelling that the workshops were kept well outside residential areas — which was the most expensive luxury textile colorant in the ancient world. To wear purple was to announce that you could afford to bleed a city's treasury for a single garment. Tyre grew fabulously wealthy on this trade.

But Tyre's wealth made it a target, and the political world of the Levant in the ninth and eighth centuries BCE was growing increasingly violent. The Assyrian empire was expanding westward, demanding tribute from the coastal cities with the casual brutality of a protection racket backed by the most efficient military machine the ancient world had yet produced. It was into this pressure cooker that Elissa was born.

---

The Story of a City

The ancient sources — primarily Virgil's Aeneid, Justin's epitome of Pompeius Trogus, and scattered references in Timaeus — tell Elissa's story in broad strokes that we cannot fully verify but cannot entirely dismiss. She was the daughter of the king of Tyre, and when her father died, the throne was meant to pass jointly to her and her brother Pygmalion. Pygmalion, hungry for sole power, murdered Elissa's husband Acerbas (also called Sychaeus), a wealthy priest of Melqart, the great Phoenician god of the city. He wanted Acerbas's gold.

Elissa took the gold and ran. She gathered around her a group of nobles who feared Pygmalion's ambitions, collected eighty young women from the temple of Astarte to serve as wives for her followers, and set sail westward. She made stops at Cyprus, where she collected additional refugees and, according to some versions, a priest of Juno whose descendants would always hold the chief priesthood at Carthage. Then she turned toward Africa.

What she founded on the hill above that bay was initially called Qart-hadasht in Phoenician — "New City," a name as utilitarian as a shipping manifest. The location was superb. The bay offered a natural harbor. The surrounding land was fertile enough to supply a city but not so rich as to make the locals militarily formidable. And the position — roughly midway along the North African coast, with Sicily visible on clear days — placed the new settlement at the geographic center of the Mediterranean's western half.

For the first century or more of its existence, Carthage was a Phoenician colony in the most literal sense: dependent on Tyre, paying an annual tribute to the mother city, modest in its ambitions. The founding myth, with its clever oxhide and its tragic queen, was probably elaborated later, when Carthage was already great and needed an origin story worthy of its greatness. Elissa herself, according to one version of the tale, died on a funeral pyre rather than submit to a forced marriage with Iarbas — a story that Virgil would borrow and transform into the tragic figure of Dido, abandoned by Aeneas and burning for love. History and poetry have been fighting over her ever since.

The transformation of Carthage from trading post to empire happened gradually, then suddenly. The trigger was the collapse of Tyre's independence. In 573 BCE, after a thirteen-year siege, Nebuchadnezzar II of Babylon finally subdued Tyre. The city survived but never recovered its old supremacy. The western Phoenician colonies were on their own.

Carthage, the largest and wealthiest of them, stepped into the role of mother city. It began absorbing the other Phoenician settlements in North Africa, Sicily, Sardinia, and Spain — sometimes by persuasion, sometimes by force. It developed a sophisticated government: two annually elected magistrates called suffetes, a powerful senate, and popular assemblies that gave it a constitutional structure the Greeks and Romans found genuinely admirable even when they were fighting for their lives against it. Aristotle, in his Politics, praised the Carthaginian constitution as one of the best in the world, noting that it had never suffered a tyranny or a major revolution.

By the fifth century BCE, Carthage controlled the most extensive commercial network in the western Mediterranean. Its merchant fleets carried grain from North Africa, silver from Spain, tin from Britain (via intermediaries), ivory and gold from sub-Saharan Africa, and the purple cloth that had always been the Phoenician signature. Its navy was the most powerful in the western sea. A Carthaginian admiral named Hanno — probably in the fifth century, though the date is contested — led an expedition of sixty ships and thousands of settlers down the Atlantic coast of Africa, founding colonies and describing, in a text that survives in Greek translation, the sight of what may have been gorillas and the glow of volcanic eruptions. A companion explorer, Himilco, sailed north toward Britain.

The city itself was extraordinary. Ancient sources describe a population of perhaps 700,000 at its height, making it one of the largest cities in the ancient world — comparable to Rome, larger than Athens. It had a double harbor, one commercial and one military, the military harbor featuring an island admiralty building from which the fleet commander could observe every ship in port. The houses, according to Appian, rose six stories high. The streets were laid out in a grid.

---

The Scholarly Debate

Here the historian must pause and wave a flag of caution, because almost everything we think we know about Carthage comes from sources that are either fragmentary or hostile.

The Carthaginians were literate. They left inscriptions — thousands of them — but almost nothing of their literature, their history, or their religious texts has survived. The great library of Carthage was apparently distributed among North African chieftains after the city's destruction in 146 BCE, and whatever they did with those books, none of it reached us. What we have instead are Greek and Roman accounts written by people who were, at various times, at war with Carthage. This is a bit like trying to reconstruct the history of Athens from Persian sources written after Marathon.

The most contested issue is the tophet — a sacred precinct found at Carthage and other Phoenician sites, filled with the cremated remains of infants and young children in urns, accompanied by votive stelae. Ancient Greek and Roman sources claimed that Carthaginians practiced child sacrifice on a massive scale, burning children alive as offerings to their gods Baal Hammon and Tanit. Modern scholars have been fiercely divided. Some argue the ancient accounts are essentially accurate. Others contend that the urns represent children who died of natural causes and were given special sacred burial, and that the "child sacrifice" narrative is enemy propaganda — the ancient equivalent of wartime atrocity stories. Recent isotopic analysis of the remains has complicated matters further, suggesting that at least some of the children in the urns were stillborn or died very young, but also that some were older, raising questions that a single explanation cannot comfortably answer. The debate remains genuinely open, which is exactly the kind of honest uncertainty good history requires.

---

The Aftermath

Carthage's rise made it the dominant power in the western Mediterranean for roughly four centuries. It fought the Greeks of Sicily to a standstill in a series of brutal wars that defined the island's history. It colonized the Iberian Peninsula so thoroughly that the region became one of its primary sources of revenue and military manpower. It projected power across the Sahara through trade networks that reached deep into sub-Saharan Africa.

And then it met Rome.

The Punic Wars — three of them, spanning from 264 to 146 BCE — are among the most consequential conflicts in ancient history. In the First Punic War, Rome seized Sicily. In the Second, Hannibal Barca crossed the Alps with his elephants and spent fifteen years ravaging Italy while Rome, with extraordinary stubbornness, refused to break. In the Third, Rome's Scipio Aemilianus besieged Carthage for three years, took it street by street in some of the most savage urban combat of the ancient world, and burned it to the ground. The story that the Romans salted the earth afterward is almost certainly a later invention, but the emotional truth it expresses is real: Rome wanted Carthage not just defeated but erased.

Erased it was — and then, paradoxically, rebuilt. Julius Caesar began a Roman colony on the site. Augustus completed it. Roman Carthage became one of the greatest cities in the empire, the capital of the province of Africa, a center of Christian theology that produced Tertullian, Cyprian, and eventually Augustine of Hippo. The city that Elissa founded with a strip of oxhide outlasted its destroyers.

---

Why It Matters

The story of Carthage's rise is, at its core, a story about what commercial civilization can build — and what it cannot always defend. Carthage was a city of merchants, and it was brilliant at the things merchants are brilliant at: logistics, negotiation, network-building, the patient accumulation of advantage. It was less reliable at the things that purely military societies are brilliant at: the willingness to absorb catastrophic losses and keep fighting, the conversion of civilian energy into military purpose, the ruthless subordination of commerce to survival.

There is something recognizable in this. Every era produces cities and states that grow powerful through trade and then discover that wealth alone does not guarantee security — that the world contains actors who do not share their values and cannot be negotiated with indefinitely. The question Carthage faced, and answered imperfectly, is one that commercial democracies have faced in every century since: how do you preserve an open, prosperous society in a world that contains people who would rather take than trade?

There is also, in the story of Carthage's erasure and our subsequent ignorance of it, a warning about the fragility of memory. We know the Punic Wars primarily through Roman eyes. The Carthaginian perspective — their literature, their history, their own account of Hannibal's genius and their city's long greatness — is gone. History, as the cliché has it, is written by the victors. But the cliché understates the damage. It is not merely that the winners get to tell the story. It is that the losers may lose the ability to tell it at all.

Elissa's oxhide trick, the purple ships, the double harbor, the six-story houses, the voyages to Africa and Britain, the constitutional government that Aristotle admired — all of it nearly vanished. That it has not vanished entirely is due to the patient work of archaeologists digging in the suburbs of modern Tunis, and to historians willing to read hostile sources against the grain. The recovery of Carthage from the rubble of Roman contempt is itself a kind of argument: that the past is worth the trouble of reconstruction, that the silenced have things to tell us, and that a city built on cleverness deserves, at minimum, a clever reading.
