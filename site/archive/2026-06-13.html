The Indus Cities and the Mystery of Harappa

The Daily Brief — Ancient History

---

A City No One Was Looking For

In the winter of 1856, a British railway engineer named John Brunton was solving a very practical problem: he needed ballast for the tracks being laid between Karachi and Lahore, and he needed a lot of it. Ballast is heavy, cheap, and ideally close at hand. One of his workers mentioned that a strange mound of old bricks rose from the flat plains near a village called Harappa, in what is now the Punjab province of Pakistan. Brunton sent men to investigate. They came back with excellent news for the railway project and, though no one understood it yet, catastrophic news for archaeology. The mound was not merely a mound. It was a city — or rather, the ghost of one — and its bricks were perfectly fired, durable, and dimensionally consistent. Hundreds of thousands of them were shoveled into the railway bed over the following months, carrying under the iron wheels of the British Empire the physical remains of a civilization that had been dead for roughly three thousand years and, until that moment, completely forgotten.

The trains ran on time. The city waited another seven decades before anyone truly looked.

---

The World Before

To understand why the Indus cities are so remarkable, you need to hold in your mind the world as it looked around 3000 BCE — not as a dark or empty place, but as a world already humming with activity and ambition.

In Mesopotamia, between the Tigris and Euphrates rivers, the Sumerians were already deep into the project of civilization: they had invented writing, built temple towers called ziggurats, and organized city-states that traded, warred, and negotiated with one another. Farther west, along the Nile, Egypt was consolidating into a unified kingdom under its earliest dynasties, raising monuments of breathtaking scale and developing a bureaucratic state that would last, in recognizable form, for three thousand years. These two civilizations — Mesopotamia and Egypt — are the ones we tend to call the "cradles" of civilization, the places where the textbooks begin.

But in the broad river plains fed by the Indus and its tributaries, something equally extraordinary was quietly taking shape. The people who would build Harappa and Mohenjo-daro were not borrowing from Sumer or Egypt. They were not a colony or an offshoot. They were doing something entirely their own, in a landscape of seasonal floods and monsoon rains, on some of the richest agricultural soil on earth. By around 2600 BCE, they would produce cities that were, in sheer urban planning and civic sophistication, arguably ahead of anything else on the planet.

The agricultural communities that preceded the great cities had been growing along the Indus Valley and its surrounding uplands for at least two thousand years before the urban period began. Villages like Mehrgarh, in what is now Balochistan, show evidence of farming, animal domestication, and craft production dating back to 7000 BCE — making this region one of the earliest agricultural zones in South Asia. Over millennia, these communities grew more complex, more interconnected, and more specialized, until sometime around 2600 BCE the threshold was crossed and cities emerged.

---

The Story of the Cities

At its height, roughly between 2600 and 1900 BCE, the Indus Valley Civilization — also called the Harappan Civilization — was the largest of the ancient world's three great urban cultures. Its territory stretched across an area of more than a million square kilometers, encompassing parts of modern-day Pakistan, northwest India, and Afghanistan. More than a thousand sites have now been identified. The two largest — Harappa in the Punjab and Mohenjo-daro on the lower Indus — each housed populations that scholars estimate between twenty thousand and fifty thousand people, making them comparable to the great cities of Mesopotamia.

What strikes every visitor to the excavated ruins, and every reader who encounters the evidence, is a quality that is almost impossible to square with our instinctive ideas about ancient life: order. Radical, almost eerie, civic order.

The streets of Mohenjo-daro were laid out on a grid. Not a rough approximation of a grid, not streets that mostly ran straight — an actual, deliberate, right-angled grid, with main avenues running roughly north-south and east-west, and smaller lanes branching off them. The houses were built of standardized fired brick, the same proportion — 1:2:4 — used consistently across the entire civilization, from the largest cities to the smallest villages, across hundreds of kilometers and hundreds of years. This is not an accident. It is evidence of a shared standard, possibly enforced by some form of authority or maintained by a deeply embedded cultural convention.

The cities had elaborate drainage systems. Every house of any substance had its own bathroom, with wastewater channels that connected to covered street drains, which in turn connected to larger municipal drains running beneath the main streets. This is, by some measures, the earliest municipal sanitation system in human history. The Romans are famous for their engineering; the Harappans were doing comparable work two thousand years before Rome was a village.

At Mohenjo-daro, archaeologists uncovered what they called the Great Bath — a large, watertight pool roughly twelve meters long and seven meters wide, lined with bitumen to prevent leakage, surrounded by colonnaded galleries, with rooms on one side that may have been changing areas or priestly quarters. No one knows exactly what the Great Bath was for. The most common interpretation is ritual purification, a function that resonates strongly with later South Asian religious traditions. Whatever its purpose, it is a feat of engineering and civic investment that speaks to a society capable of organizing collective labor for non-subsistence ends — the hallmark of what we call civilization.

The Harappans were also remarkable traders. Their artifacts — distinctive carved stone seals, beads of carnelian and lapis lazuli, copper tools — have been found in Mesopotamian sites, suggesting active commerce across the Persian Gulf and possibly overland through what is now Iran and Afghanistan. Mesopotamian texts refer to a land called Meluhha, thought by many scholars to be the Indus region, from which ships brought exotic goods. The Harappans were not isolated. They were participants in the ancient world's first long-distance trade networks.

And yet — and here the mystery begins to deepen — almost nothing about them is legible to us.

---

The Mystery

The Indus script is one of the great unsolved puzzles of human history. More than four thousand inscribed objects have been recovered, mostly the small carved seals that seem to have been used in trade or administration. The signs are clearly a writing system — they appear in consistent sequences, they have a defined repertoire of symbols, they show all the structural hallmarks of language encoded in signs. And yet, after more than a century of effort by some of the finest minds in linguistics and cryptography, the script has not been deciphered.

The reason is brutal in its simplicity: we have no bilingual text. The Rosetta Stone unlocked Egyptian hieroglyphics because it said the same thing in three scripts, one of which was already known. For the Indus script, we have no such key. We do not know what language — or languages — underlies the symbols. We do not know whether the inscriptions are names, titles, prayers, commercial records, or something else entirely. The texts are also very short; the average inscription is fewer than five signs. This is not enough data to crack a code in isolation.

This silence extends beyond the writing. We do not know what the Harappans called themselves. We do not know the names of their cities, their rulers, their gods. We have no royal tomb, no palace definitively identified as such, no monument bearing the name of a king. The absence of obvious palaces and elaborate royal burials is itself one of the most debated features of the civilization. Mesopotamia and Egypt are defined in part by their rulers — the pharaoh, the lugal, the king who builds monuments to his own glory. Harappan archaeology has produced nothing comparable.

This has led to genuinely fascinating scholarly debate. Some researchers argue that the Harappans were governed not by kings but by some form of merchant oligarchy or priestly council — a civic rather than monarchical system. Others suggest that the great "citadels" found at major cities (raised platforms, possibly fortified or at least elevated above the lower town) represent some form of administrative or religious authority that we simply haven't been able to read correctly. Still others caution against projecting our expectations at all: perhaps the Harappans organized power in ways that have no good parallel in our existing categories.

The question of their identity has also become entangled in modern politics. The debate over whether the Harappans spoke an early form of Sanskrit — and were thus ancestors of the later Vedic culture — or whether they spoke a Dravidian language ancestral to the languages of South India today is not merely academic. It touches on deeply contested questions about who has the deepest roots in the Indian subcontinent, questions that carry enormous political charge in modern India and Pakistan. Scholars have been passionate, sometimes intemperate, and the evidence remains genuinely ambiguous.

---

The Decline and What Came After

Around 1900 BCE, something began to go wrong. The great cities were gradually abandoned. The standardized brick, the elaborate drainage, the long-distance trade — all of it faded over roughly three to four centuries. By around 1500 BCE, the urban phase of the Indus civilization was over.

The cause of the decline has been debated for decades. Early theories blamed an invasion — specifically, the arrival of Indo-Aryan-speaking peoples from Central Asia, who some scholars linked to the later Vedic culture. The archaeologist Mortimer Wheeler, who excavated Mohenjo-daro in the 1940s with characteristic dramatic flair, famously pointed to skeletons found in the upper levels of the city and suggested they were massacre victims. This "Aryan invasion" theory has largely been abandoned or heavily qualified; the skeletal evidence does not support a sudden violent end, and the decline appears too gradual and geographically variable for a single catastrophic event.

The current scholarly consensus leans toward a combination of environmental factors. Climate data and geological evidence suggest that the monsoon patterns shifted around this period, becoming more erratic and less reliable. The rivers themselves may have changed course — a catastrophic event in an agricultural society utterly dependent on predictable flooding and irrigation. There is also evidence of significant flooding at Mohenjo-daro itself, suggesting that the same rivers that gave the civilization life could also destroy it.

What followed is not a blank. The Harappan people did not vanish. They dispersed. Smaller settlements in the eastern regions — toward what is now Gujarat and the Gangetic plain — actually grew during and after the urban decline, suggesting population movement rather than extinction. The material culture changed, simplified, regionalized. And somewhere in this long transition, the cultural threads that would eventually weave together into the rich tapestry of South Asian civilization — its languages, its religious ideas, its social structures — were being knotted together from multiple sources, Harappan and otherwise.

The Vedic culture that emerges in the historical record from around 1500 BCE onward is not a replacement of the Harappan world so much as something that grew partly from its ruins and partly from new arrivals, a palimpsest of civilizations written over one another on the same fertile ground.

---

Why It Matters

There is a temptation, when we encounter a civilization we cannot read, to treat it as lesser — as proto-history, as archaeology rather than history, as interesting but ultimately peripheral to the story we already know. The Indus cities resist this condescension.

They matter, first, because they remind us how much of human achievement has been lost. We are not reading the full record of the past; we are reading the fragments that survived, and the Harappan script stands as a monument to everything we do not know. Humility is not weakness in historical thinking — it is precision.

They matter because the absence of obvious kings and monuments to personal glory raises a genuinely important question: must civilization require hierarchy and domination to function? The Harappan cities were large, sophisticated, and apparently stable for centuries. Whatever held them together did not leave us triumphal arches or royal portraits. Perhaps it did not need to.

They matter because the political debates around Harappan identity — who owns this past, whose ancestors these people were — are a living demonstration that history is never merely about the past. The story of Harappa is being written and rewritten today in ways that shape how hundreds of millions of people understand their own origins.

And they matter, perhaps most of all, because they remind us that the story of human civilization is not a single river flowing from one source, but a delta — braided, complex, sometimes disappearing underground only to resurface somewhere unexpected. The bricks that went into a Victorian railway bed were not just rubble. They were someone's city, someone's home, someone's world. We owe it our attention.
