The City Nobody Fully Understands

Teotihuacan and the Riddle at the Heart of Ancient Mesoamerica

The Daily Brief — Ancient History

---

Imagine standing at the base of the Pyramid of the Sun at dawn. The structure rises 216 feet above the Valley of Mexico, its five stepped tiers catching the first pale light before the surrounding mountains do. You are not a tourist with a guidebook. You are a merchant from Oaxaca, perhaps, or a diplomat from the Maya lowlands, arriving after weeks of travel along roads that converge on this place from every direction. You have heard about this city your entire life. Now you are inside it, and it is larger than anything you believed a human community could be. The avenue stretching before you is wider than a modern highway, flanked by temples and apartment compounds that house tens of thousands of people. The air smells of copal smoke, roasting maize, and the distant tang of obsidian dust from the workshops that never seem to stop. Somewhere ahead, at the avenue's northern terminus, another pyramid looms — the Moon — framed perfectly by the mountain called Cerro Gordo, as though the city's architects had borrowed the horizon itself as a backdrop.

You do not know who built this place. Neither, in a very honest sense, do we.

---

The World Before

To understand Teotihuacan, you have to understand how unusual it was — and that requires a brief look at what preceded it.

By the first century BCE, Mesoamerica was already ancient. The Olmec had raised colossal stone heads along the Gulf Coast a thousand years earlier. The Zapotec were building Monte Albán in the Oaxacan highlands. The Maya were beginning to experiment with monumental architecture and the writing system that would eventually record their kings' genealogies in extraordinary detail. Scattered across the Basin of Mexico — the broad highland plateau ringed by volcanoes that would one day hold Aztec Tenochtitlan and, later, Mexico City — were dozens of competing towns and villages, none of them dominant, all of them jostling for agricultural land, trade routes, and prestige.

Then Cuicuilco, the largest settlement in the basin at the time, was buried under lava from the Xitle volcano sometime around 100 BCE. The catastrophe was gradual enough that most inhabitants escaped with their lives, but the city was finished. Refugees streamed northward. And in the northeastern corner of the basin, near a cave that locals apparently considered a portal to the underworld, a settlement that had been growing for a century or more suddenly began to grow very, very fast.

That settlement would become Teotihuacan.

---

The Story of a City

The name "Teotihuacan" is not what its inhabitants called it. It is a Nahuatl word applied by the Aztecs centuries after the city had already fallen — meaning something roughly like "the place where men become gods" or "the birthplace of the gods." The Aztecs regarded the ruined city with a mixture of awe and reverence so profound that they incorporated it into their own mythology. They believed the current sun and moon had been created there in a cosmic sacrifice. They made pilgrimages to the site. They buried their own offerings in its ancient structures. But they did not know who had built it, and they said so.

Neither do we. The inhabitants of Teotihuacan left no deciphered writing. Unlike the Maya, who carved their kings' names and deeds onto stelae and lintels with almost compulsive thoroughness, Teotihuacan's rulers — if individual rulers there were — remain nameless. We cannot say with certainty what language or languages the city's people spoke, though Nahuatl, Totonac, and proto-Totonacan have all been proposed. We do not know what they called their city, their gods, or themselves.

What we do know is the scale of what they built, and the speed with which they built it.

By roughly 100 CE, the Pyramid of the Sun was already standing — one of the largest structures in the pre-Columbian world, constructed in a remarkably short period, its orientation aligned to the point on the western horizon where the Pleiades set, or possibly to the cave directly beneath it, or possibly both. The Street of the Dead — another Aztec name — ran for more than two miles through the city's ceremonial core. The Pyramid of the Moon anchored the northern end. The Ciudadela, a vast sunken plaza large enough to hold the city's entire adult population at once, anchored the southern. At the center of the Ciudadela stood the Temple of the Feathered Serpent, its facade covered in carved serpent heads alternating with what may be a storm deity, the whole thing painted in brilliant reds, greens, and yellows that we can only partially reconstruct today.

Beneath that temple, archaeologists in the 1980s and 1990s found something that changed how scholars thought about the city: mass burials. Dozens of individuals, many of them apparently warriors bound with their hands behind their backs, sacrificed at the temple's dedication. They wore necklaces made of human maxillae — jaw bones — and were accompanied by obsidian projectile points and pyrite mirrors. The scale and apparent organization of the sacrifices suggested not a city of peaceful merchants and pilgrims, as an earlier generation of scholars had romantically imagined, but a state capable of organized violence on a considerable scale.

At its height, between roughly 100 and 550 CE, Teotihuacan may have housed 100,000 to 200,000 people. That made it one of the largest cities on Earth at the time, comparable to imperial Rome and Han dynasty Luoyang. It was organized in an almost urban-planner's grid, with apartment compounds — each housing perhaps sixty to one hundred people in interconnected rooms arranged around interior courtyards — stretching for miles in every direction from the ceremonial core. The compounds were not hovels. Many were painted with elaborate murals depicting processions of figures, agricultural abundance, and what appear to be ritual ceremonies. The people who lived in them ate well by ancient standards, had access to obsidian tools, and seem to have participated in a civic life whose contours we are still tracing.

The city's influence radiated outward in ways that still astonish. Teotihuacan-style pottery and architecture appear at sites hundreds of miles away. At Tikal, one of the great Maya cities of the Guatemalan lowlands, a monument records that in 378 CE a figure connected to Teotihuacan — possibly an envoy, possibly a conqueror — arrived and apparently installed a new dynastic order. Scholars debate vigorously whether this represents military conquest, elite intermarriage, diplomatic alliance, or the adoption of prestigious foreign symbols by ambitious local rulers. But the Teotihuacan connection at Tikal is real, carved in stone, and it is just one of dozens of such connections found across Mesoamerica. This was not a city that sat quietly in its valley. It reached.

---

The Scholarly Debate

The central mystery of Teotihuacan is governance, and it is a genuine mystery, not a rhetorical one.

Most ancient cities of comparable size were ruled by kings — individuals whose names and faces we can identify, whose palaces we can locate, whose ideology centered on the divine right of a single lineage to rule. Teotihuacan appears to be different. Decades of excavation have failed to produce a clear royal palace. The murals, so abundant throughout the city, almost never depict an individual ruler in the triumphant postures common at Maya sites. The sacrificial deposits at the Temple of the Feathered Serpent suggest state power, but state power exercised by whom?

Some scholars, most notably Esther Pasztory, have proposed that Teotihuacan was organized around a collective or corporate ideology — that power was deliberately distributed among a ruling council or a group of elite lineages, and that the city's art avoided glorifying individuals precisely because the political system was built on the suppression of individual aggrandizement. Others find this too neat, arguing that absence of evidence is not evidence of absence, and that the royal palace simply hasn't been found yet — or was destroyed, or identified as something else. Recent excavations at a compound called Xalla have produced evidence of a high-status residential complex that may have housed elites capable of something like royal authority.

Then there is the question of ethnicity and origin. Teotihuacan's population was clearly diverse. Distinct neighborhoods have been identified for Oaxacan residents, for people from the Gulf Coast, possibly for Maya-affiliated groups. Was this a multiethnic empire? A cosmopolitan trade hub? A sacred city that drew pilgrims and merchants from across Mesoamerica? The honest answer is: probably all of these things, in different proportions at different times.

And then the city fell.

---

The Collapse and What Rose From It

Around 550 CE — the precise date is uncertain and probably varied by neighborhood — Teotihuacan experienced a catastrophic collapse. The evidence is stark: the ceremonial core of the city was burned. Not accidentally. The fires were selective, concentrated on the temples, the monuments, the structures most associated with civic and religious authority. Personal residences were largely spared. This was targeted destruction, and it was thorough.

Who did it? Again, the honest answer is that we do not know. The destruction may have been internal — a popular uprising against an oppressive elite, a civic revolution that burned the symbols of power. It may have been external — raiders or rivals from outside the basin, taking advantage of a city weakened by drought, resource depletion, or political fracture. Recent paleoclimatological data suggests that prolonged drought in the sixth century CE may have stressed the agricultural systems that fed the city's enormous population, creating the conditions for whatever catastrophe followed. Most likely the collapse was overdetermined: multiple stresses converging until the system broke.

The city did not empty overnight. People continued to live in Teotihuacan for centuries after the burning of the ceremonial center. But the population shrank dramatically, the apartment compounds fell into disrepair, and the city's extraordinary influence over the rest of Mesoamerica evaporated.

What rose from it was, in a sense, everything that came after. The political vacuum left by Teotihuacan's collapse reshaped the Basin of Mexico and beyond. New powers emerged — Xochicalco, Cacaxtla, Tula — each of them in some way inheriting, imitating, or reacting against the Teotihuacan template. The Toltec state that rose at Tula in the ninth and tenth centuries carried Teotihuacan's imagery and probably its mythology into a new era. And when the Aztecs built their empire in the fourteenth and fifteenth centuries, they made deliberate pilgrimages to the ruined pyramids, buried offerings in the ancient structures, and wove the dead city into the founding mythology of their own civilization.

The Aztecs understood, intuitively, that they were living in the long shadow of something they could not fully explain. In this respect, they were not so different from us.

---

Why It Matters

Teotihuacan forces a confrontation with one of the most persistent assumptions in how we think about large, successful civilizations: the assumption that they must have been organized the way we expect powerful things to be organized — with a single leader at the top, a clear hierarchy descending from that leader, and a story about that leader at the center of everything.

The city's stubborn resistance to that narrative is not a failure of archaeology. It may be an accurate reflection of a genuinely different political imagination — one in which collective governance, civic ideology, and distributed authority produced a city that lasted five centuries and shaped a continent, without leaving us a single royal name to attach to the achievement.

That is a remarkable thing to sit with. In an age saturated with arguments about leadership, hierarchy, and the supposed necessity of strongman politics, a city of perhaps 150,000 people that may have been governed by something closer to a council than a king — and that left behind apartment compounds with painted walls and equitable access to resources that many ancient cities did not provide — is worth more than a footnote. It is worth the kind of sustained, honest attention that admits what we do not know while taking seriously what the evidence does show.

We are still learning how to read Teotihuacan. New excavations, new imaging technologies, new analyses of isotopes in ancient bones are producing new answers every decade. But the city's deepest lesson may already be available to anyone willing to stand at the base of the Pyramid of the Sun and ask, genuinely, who built this — and then sit with the uncertainty of the answer.

Some of the most important things humans have ever done were done by people whose names we will never know.
