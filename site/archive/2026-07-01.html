The Daily Brief

A Journey That Changed the World: The Hijra and the Birth of the Muslim Community

Wednesday — Medieval Arabia

---

The Road Out of Mecca

Imagine a man in his early fifties, a merchant well known to every trader and pilgrim in his city, crouching in a cave on a hillside south of Mecca while his pursuers pass close enough that he can hear their sandals scraping against the rock. He has been a respected figure in this city for decades. He has buried children here. His first wife, the woman who believed in him before anyone else, lies in its soil. And yet the city has turned against him so completely that staying means death.

The year, by the reckoning scholars would later settle on, is 622 CE. The man is Muhammad ibn Abdullah. The cave is Thawr. And the journey he is about to undertake — northward across roughly 320 kilometers of desert and volcanic plateau to the oasis town of Yathrib — will become one of the most consequential migrations in human history. Muslims call it the Hijra, the emigration, and they would eventually mark it as the beginning of their calendar: Year One.

To understand why this journey mattered so much that a civilization chose to count time from it, you have to understand the world it interrupted.

---

The World Before

Arabia in the early seventh century CE was not a backwater, though outsiders sometimes imagined it as one. It sat at the hinge of enormous commercial systems. Caravans out of Yemen carried frankincense and myrrh northward to the Byzantine and Sassanid empires. Indian spices and Chinese silk arrived by sea at the ports of the Red Sea coast and were redistributed overland. Mecca itself owed its wealth and its importance to this traffic — and to the Kaaba, a cube-shaped shrine at the city's heart that housed, by some accounts, over three hundred idols representing the deities of dozens of Arabian tribes. The Quraysh tribe, Muhammad's own people, were the custodians of the Kaaba and the managers of the annual pilgrimage that brought tribes from across the peninsula together each year in a kind of sacred truce.

This was a world organized around kinship. Your tribe was your social security, your legal system, your army, and your identity. Blood feuds could last generations. A man without tribal protection was almost legally invisible — he could be killed without consequence. Women's status varied enormously by region and class, but the overall structure favored men of powerful lineages. Slavery was ubiquitous. The gap between the wealthy merchants of Mecca and the poor, the enslaved, and the socially marginal was vast and growing.

Into this world, Muhammad had been preaching for roughly twelve years by 622 CE. The message he proclaimed — received, he said, through the angel Gabriel — was radical in its implications even if it built on traditions already familiar from Judaism and Christianity. There was one God, not hundreds. All human beings stood equal before that God regardless of tribe or lineage. Wealth hoarded while neighbors starved was a spiritual failure. The powerful would be held accountable. The enslaved and the poor were not invisible to the divine.

The Quraysh leadership understood very clearly what this meant for their social order — and for the pilgrimage economy built on the plurality of gods. They had tried mockery, then economic boycott, then violence. Companions of Muhammad were tortured. Bilal ibn Rabah, an enslaved man from Abyssinia who had become one of the earliest converts, was stretched on burning sand with a boulder on his chest to force him to recant. He refused. The early Muslim community in Mecca was small, harassed, and increasingly in danger. Some had already fled to the Christian kingdom of Aksum in Abyssinia, where the Negus gave them shelter — a fact Muslims have long remembered as a moment of interfaith protection.

But Mecca itself was becoming untenable. When Muhammad's uncle and protector Abu Talib died, followed shortly by his wife Khadijah, he lost both his political shield and his emotional anchor in the same terrible year. Without tribal protection, he was vulnerable in a way that the Quraysh now moved to exploit.

---

The Migration Itself

The plan was carefully laid. Most of Muhammad's followers had already slipped out of Mecca in small groups over the preceding weeks, making their way to Yathrib, where a series of negotiations — the pledges of Aqaba — had secured the promise of welcome and protection from several of the town's tribes. Muhammad himself waited, knowing the Quraysh were watching him. According to Islamic tradition, the night he left, he asked his young cousin Ali ibn Abi Talib to sleep in his bed as a decoy.

He left with a single companion: Abu Bakr, his closest friend and one of the earliest believers. They traveled south first, away from Yathrib, to throw off pursuit — a counterintuitive move that required nerve. At the cave of Thawr they hid for three days while search parties combed the area. The Quraysh had reportedly offered a reward of a hundred camels for Muhammad's capture, dead or alive.

Then they turned north, taking a coastal route unfamiliar to most travelers rather than the main road. A guide named Abdullah ibn Urayqit, who was not yet a Muslim, led them across the terrain. They traveled mostly at night. After roughly two weeks of extraordinary tension, they arrived at Quba, a settlement on the outskirts of Yathrib, on the twelfth day of the month of Rabi' al-Awwal — a date that would later become the subject of considerable scholarly and religious attention.

The welcome was, by the accounts preserved in early Islamic sources, overwhelming. People lined the road. Children sang. The town had been fractured for years by conflict between its Arab tribes — the Aws and the Khazraj — and its significant Jewish communities. Muhammad arrived not merely as a prophet but as an agreed-upon arbitrator, a figure around whom a new kind of community might cohere.

---

Building Something New: Yathrib Becomes Medina

What happened in Yathrib over the next decade is, depending on your perspective, a story of religious community formation, early state-building, or both simultaneously — and the tension between those interpretations remains alive among scholars today.

Almost immediately, Muhammad established a mosque — not yet a grand building but a simple courtyard with a palm-trunk colonnade — and began the work of binding together a community that was strikingly diverse. There were the Muhajirun, the emigrants who had come with him from Mecca, many of whom had left behind everything. There were the Ansar, the "helpers," the Yathrib converts who welcomed them. There were Arab tribes who had not yet converted and Jewish tribes who maintained their own faith. The town, which came to be called Madinat al-Nabi — the city of the Prophet, shortened to Medina — needed a framework for all of them.

The document that emerged is known to scholars as the Constitution of Medina, or the Sahifa. It is one of the most remarkable texts of early medieval history. Stipulating the rights and obligations of the various communities in the new polity, it established that the Jewish tribes of Medina were a community alongside the Muslims, that all parties had mutual defense obligations, that disputes would be referred to Muhammad for arbitration, and that no one would make a separate peace with the Quraysh. Whether this document represents an early proto-constitution, a confederation treaty, or something else entirely, historians continue to debate. What is striking is how practical it is — this was a working political document, not a purely theological one.

The city of the Prophet became a laboratory. The five daily prayers were formalized. The adhan, the call to prayer, was instituted — and Bilal, the formerly enslaved man who had been tortured for his faith in Mecca, was chosen to be the first muezzin, his powerful voice carrying across the rooftops of Medina in an act that was surely understood as a statement about what this community was meant to be. The direction of prayer, the qibla, shifted from Jerusalem to Mecca, a change that signaled both the community's relationship to earlier Abrahamic traditions and its assertion of a distinct identity.

---

The Scholarly Debate

Historians examining this period face a fundamental challenge: the earliest written sources come from roughly a century and a half after the events they describe. The sira — the biography of the Prophet — tradition was compiled by scholars like Ibn Ishaq (died c. 767 CE) working from oral transmission chains. The hadith literature, the collected sayings and actions of Muhammad, was systematically evaluated and compiled even later. This creates real uncertainty about which details are historically reliable, which have been shaped by the theological and political concerns of later periods, and which may be legendary accretions.

Western academic scholars from the nineteenth century onward — figures like Ignaz Goldziher and, more controversially in recent decades, Patricia Crone and Michael Cook in their 1977 work Hagarism — have argued that the Islamic tradition about its own origins is substantially a later construction, and that early Islam looked rather different from the polished narrative preserved in classical sources. These arguments have been vigorously contested, and the field has moved considerably since Crone and Cook's early provocations; Crone herself revised many of her positions substantially. The discovery and analysis of documentary papyri, early Quranic manuscripts (including the Birmingham and Sana'a manuscripts), and non-Islamic contemporary sources have given scholars new tools. The emerging picture is one of a community that was genuinely novel and rapidly evolving, in active conversation with the Jewish and Christian traditions around it, and whose self-understanding was being worked out in real time.

What does seem clear, even to skeptical historians, is that within a decade of the Hijra, something unprecedented had coalesced: a community that identified itself by religious rather than purely tribal bonds, that had developed durable institutions, and that was capable of sustained military, diplomatic, and organizational action on a scale that would astonish the world.

---

The Aftermath

Muhammad died in Medina in 632 CE, ten years after the Hijra. He died having returned to Mecca in triumph, having cleared the Kaaba of its idols, and having seen much of the Arabian Peninsula nominally unified under the banner of Islam. What followed was explosive in ways that no one alive at the time of that cave-side escape could plausibly have imagined.

Within a generation, Arab armies had conquered the Sassanid Persian Empire entirely and stripped the Byzantines of Egypt, Syria, and Palestine. Within a century, the Islamic world stretched from the Iberian Peninsula to the borders of Tang China. The Arabic language, carried on the back of the Quran's insistence on its own untranslatability, became a lingua franca for science, philosophy, medicine, and commerce across an enormous arc of the world. The institutions pioneered in Medina — the mosque as community center, the concept of the umma or community of believers that transcended tribe and ethnicity, the integration of law and religious practice — became templates for civilization.

The calendar that begins with the Hijra is itself a statement of values. It does not begin with Muhammad's birth, or with the first revelation, or with the conquest of Mecca. It begins with the moment the community formed — with the act of migration toward something rather than the glory of triumph. That choice of starting point speaks volumes about what the tradition considered essential.

---

Why It Matters

The Hijra is, at its core, a story about what happens when a small, persecuted community decides to build something new rather than simply survive. It raises questions that feel urgently contemporary: How do you create solidarity across lines of tribe, class, and prior enmity? What does it mean to found a community on shared belief rather than shared blood? How do political necessity and spiritual vision interact — do they corrupt each other, or can they be held together? What obligations do the powerful owe to the vulnerable within a community?

The Medinan experiment was messy, contested, and imperfect — as all human institutions are. Relations with the Jewish tribes of Medina deteriorated badly in the years that followed, culminating in violence that Islamic tradition itself records with discomfort. The question of who would lead after Muhammad produced the Sunni-Shia division that remains one of the great fault lines of the modern world. The ideals proclaimed at the founding were never fully realized.

And yet the founding ideals were proclaimed. The idea that a community could choose its identity rather than simply inherit it, that the formerly enslaved could call the community to prayer, that a document might govern relations between communities of different faiths — these were real innovations with real consequences. They echoed through centuries of Islamic law, philosophy, and political thought, and they continue to echo today in the arguments that Muslims around the world have with each other and with their history about what their tradition actually demands of them.

Every community that has ever migrated toward a better version of itself — every group that has left behind the familiar in pursuit of something it believed in — is in some sense walking the road from Mecca to Medina.
