The Last War of the Ancient World

How Persia and Byzantium Fought Each Other to Exhaustion — and Opened the Door to Islam

---

The Daily Brief — Late Antiquity | West Asia

---

The Hook

In the spring of 614, soldiers dragged the True Cross out of the Church of the Holy Sepulchre in Jerusalem. They were Persians, servants of the Sasanian King of Kings, Khosrow II, and they carried Christendom's most sacred relic eastward across the desert toward Ctesiphon as a trophy of war. The city of Jerusalem itself burned behind them. Tens of thousands were dead or enslaved. The Roman Emperor Heraclius, sitting in Constantinople, reportedly wept when the news arrived. A court poet compared the moment to the fall of Troy.

What followed over the next two decades would be one of the most dramatic reversals in the history of warfare — a story of near-annihilation, impossible comeback, and ultimate ruin for both sides. By the time it was over, two of the greatest empires the ancient world had ever produced lay shattered, their treasuries empty, their armies bled white, their populations exhausted and plague-ridden. Neither would ever fully recover. Into the vacuum they left, something entirely new was about to rush.

---

The World Before

To understand what was at stake, you have to understand how old the rivalry was.

For six centuries, the Roman world and its successor states had faced a great power to the east. First it was the Parthians, then, from 224 CE onward, the Sasanians — a dynasty that considered itself the legitimate heir of the ancient Achaemenid empire of Cyrus and Darius. The Sasanians were not barbarians at the gate. They were the other superpower of Late Antiquity: builders of magnificent cities, patrons of philosophy, administrators of a complex bureaucracy, and practitioners of Zoroastrianism, a faith with its own rich theology of cosmic struggle between light and darkness.

Their capital, Ctesiphon, straddled the Tigris in what is now central Iraq. Its great audience hall, the Taq Kasra — a vaulted arch so immense that it still stands today, the largest unreinforced brick arch in human history — announced to every visitor that the King of Kings was a ruler of cosmic stature. The Sasanian court was a world of elaborate ceremony, silk, and silver, of fire temples and royal hunts, of a landed aristocracy that provided heavily armored cavalry called savaran who were among the most formidable soldiers of the age.

The Byzantines, for their part, were the continuation of Rome's eastern half, Greek-speaking, Christian, and fiercely proud of an imperial tradition stretching back to Augustus. Constantinople, built on a peninsula commanding the Bosphorus, was the largest and wealthiest city in the Western world, its walls having never been breached. The two empires had fought each other across Mesopotamia, Armenia, and Syria for generations, signing treaties, exchanging hostages, trading silk, and then going to war again. It was a rivalry so entrenched that contemporaries could barely imagine a world without it.

By the early seventh century, both empires were fraying. Decades of plague — the Justinianic Plague had ravaged the Mediterranean world since the 540s — had hollowed out populations and tax revenues. The Byzantines had fought exhausting campaigns against the Avars and Slavs on the Danube frontier. The Sasanians had their own dynastic instabilities. When Khosrow II came to power in 590, he had actually done so with Byzantine military assistance — the Emperor Maurice had helped restore him to his throne. For a brief, strange moment, the two empires were almost allies.

Then, in 602, a brutal military mutiny on the Danube produced a new Byzantine emperor: Phocas, a centurion who murdered Maurice and his sons and seized Constantinople. Khosrow used the murder of his patron as a pretext. He declared war to avenge Maurice. Whether he genuinely grieved the man or simply recognized an opportunity, historians still debate. But the result was the same: the last and most catastrophic war between the two ancient superpowers had begun.

---

The Story Itself

The early years of the war went catastrophically for Byzantium. Khosrow's armies, led by brilliant generals — most notably a Christian Persian commander named Shahrbaraz — swept through the eastern provinces with a speed that seemed almost supernatural to those in their path. Syria fell. Antioch, one of the great cities of the ancient world, fell. In 614, Jerusalem fell, the True Cross taken, the Patriarch carried off to Persia, the Holy Sepulchre itself damaged by fire. The psychological shock to the Byzantine world was immense. This was not merely a military defeat; it felt, to a deeply Christian civilization, like divine abandonment.

Egypt fell in 619. This was perhaps the single most economically devastating blow. Egypt was the breadbasket of the empire, the source of the grain that fed Constantinople. Its loss threatened the capital with starvation. By around 620, Persian armies had reached Chalcedon — directly across the Bosphorus from Constantinople itself. A Persian soldier could have stood on the Asian shore and looked across the water at the Golden Horn.

Meanwhile, a new emperor had emerged in Constantinople. Heraclius had deposed and executed Phocas in 610, arriving by sea from Carthage. He was a capable administrator but, in those early years, a cautious military commander. He reportedly considered abandoning Constantinople entirely and moving the capital to Carthage. He did not. Instead, he spent years reorganizing, re-equipping, and re-inspiring his army while negotiating a humiliating truce with the Avars on his western flank.

What happened next is one of the great stories of military history. In 622, Heraclius launched a counteroffensive of startling audacity. Rather than fight the Persians on the conventional fronts in Syria or Mesopotamia, he loaded his army onto ships and sailed through the Bosphorus into the Black Sea, landing in Anatolia and then marching into the Caucasus — striking at Persia from an unexpected angle, threatening Armenia and Azerbaijan, the heartland of Zoroastrian fire temples.

Over the next several years, Heraclius conducted a series of campaigns that modern historians have compared to Napoleon's style of warfare: rapid movement, living off the land, seeking decisive battle rather than siege. He won engagements at Issus and in Armenia. He struck deep into Persian-held territory. He destroyed the great fire temple at Ganzak (Shiz), in what is now northwestern Iran, a desecration as symbolically devastating to Persians as the taking of the True Cross had been to Christians.

The crisis came in 626. With Heraclius deep in the east, Khosrow gambled on a knockout blow: a coordinated siege of Constantinople by both the Persian army (crossing from the Asian shore) and the Avar Khaganate attacking from Europe. It was a brilliant strategic conception — the city caught between two armies, Heraclius too far away to return in time. The siege began in late July.

It failed. The Byzantine navy, controlling the Bosphorus, prevented the Persians and Avars from coordinating. The Avar assault was beaten back from the walls. Constantinople held. The Persians withdrew. The Avars, humiliated, retreated north. And Heraclius, receiving news of the city's deliverance, pressed his advantage in the east.

In 627, Heraclius fought what may have been the decisive engagement of the entire war: the Battle of Nineveh, near the ruins of the ancient Assyrian capital in what is now northern Iraq. The Persian general Rhahzadh was killed in the fighting — according to some sources, by Heraclius himself in single combat, though historians treat that detail with appropriate skepticism. The Persian army broke. Heraclius advanced toward Ctesiphon.

Khosrow II, the great king who had seemed on the verge of re-creating the ancient Persian empire, was now a fugitive in his own realm. His generals and nobles had seen enough. In early 628, his own son, Kavad II, led a coup. Khosrow was imprisoned and then killed — reportedly shot with arrows, slowly, over several days, a death designed to humiliate as much as destroy. Kavad immediately sued for peace. The True Cross was returned. Byzantine prisoners were released. The conquered provinces were handed back.

Heraclius returned to Constantinople in triumph. He personally restored the True Cross to Jerusalem in 630, walking barefoot into the city in a gesture of imperial piety. It must have seemed like one of history's great vindications.

---

The Scholarly Debate

Historians have long debated the nature and significance of this conflict in ways that go beyond simple military history.

One major question concerns Heraclius himself. Was he the strategic genius that traditional historiography describes, or was he an improviser who got lucky? The classicist Walter Kaegi, who has written the most thorough modern study of the emperor, argues for a more nuanced view: Heraclius was genuinely innovative in his use of the Caucasian theater and in his ability to motivate troops through religious symbolism — he framed the war explicitly as a holy war, a concept that was new and electrifying for Byzantine armies — but he also benefited enormously from Persian overextension and internal dysfunction.

Another debate concerns the religious dimension. Heraclius's framing of the conflict as a war for Christendom is often cited as one of the first instances of what we might recognize as holy war ideology in the Christian tradition. Some scholars see this as a desperate propaganda move; others see it as a genuine theological development that would echo forward into the Crusades.

There is also the question of what the war did to the populations caught in the middle. The Syriac and Armenian Christian communities of the Near East experienced the Persian occupation in complex ways — some sources suggest that Persian rule was not uniformly brutal, and that Jewish communities in Palestine may have initially welcomed the Persians as an alternative to Byzantine religious pressure. The picture is messier and more human than simple narratives of Persian aggression allow.

---

The Aftermath

The peace of 628 looked like a Byzantine triumph. It was not. It was a mutual catastrophe dressed in the costume of victory.

Both empires had spent three decades in nearly continuous warfare. Tax revenues had been devastated. Agricultural land had been abandoned or destroyed. Populations had been displaced, enslaved, or killed. The plague continued its slow work. The Byzantine army that returned from the east was formidable but exhausted. The Sasanian state collapsed into a decade of civil war after Kavad II's death — between 628 and 632, no fewer than twelve rulers claimed the Persian throne. The machinery of empire ground to a halt.

In 632, a man named Muhammad died in the Arabian city of Medina. He had spent the last decade building a new religious community and political order among the Arab tribes of the Hejaz. His successors — the first caliphs — launched military expeditions northward into the exhausted borderlands between the two empires. What they found was not what anyone expected.

The Arab armies that defeated the Byzantines at the Battle of the Yarmuk in 636 and destroyed the last Sasanian field army at al-Qadisiyyah around the same time were not fighting the empires at their height. They were fighting the hollow shells of two civilizations that had spent themselves destroying each other. Syria, Palestine, and Egypt — the very provinces whose populations had lived through Persian occupation and Byzantine reconquest within a single generation — offered surprisingly little resistance to the new conquerors. The Sasanian Empire did not merely lose territory; it ceased to exist entirely within two decades of Yarmuk.

Heraclius lived long enough to see Syria and Egypt lost again — this time permanently. He died in 641, reportedly so distressed by the Arab conquests that he could barely speak. The True Cross, which he had restored to Jerusalem with such ceremony just eleven years earlier, was hidden away and lost to history.

---

Why It Matters

There is a temptation to see the Byzantine-Sasanian War as a footnote — a prelude to the "real" story of the Islamic conquests. That reading gets things backward. The war was the story. The Islamic conquests were, in significant part, its consequence.

What the last great war of antiquity teaches us is something about the structural fragility of even the most powerful states. Two empires that had dominated their world for centuries, that possessed sophisticated administrative systems and battle-hardened armies, destroyed each other's capacity to survive through sheer persistence. Neither side could accept a negotiated equilibrium. Both sides kept fighting past the point of strategic rationality. The result was a power vacuum that neither had imagined possible.

There is also something here about the relationship between military victory and political survival. Heraclius won his war. He recovered every lost province. He humiliated Khosrow. And yet within a decade, everything he had won was gone — not because he had fought badly, but because the act of winning had left nothing with which to hold the prize.

The story also reminds us how quickly the "eternal" can become the historical. The Sasanian Empire, which had existed for four centuries and considered itself the heir of a tradition stretching back to Cyrus the Great, was simply gone by 651. Its fire temples were converted. Its language was transformed by Arabic. Its last king, Yazdegerd III, was murdered by a miller near Merv, alone and unrecognized, the end of a dynasty that had once made Rome tremble.

The world before the Islamic conquests was not a world waiting patiently for them to happen. It was a world that had, through its own violence and exhaustion, made them possible. That distinction matters — because it suggests that history is rarely the story of inevitable forces, but almost always the story of contingent choices, made by specific people, whose consequences spiral outward in ways no one predicted.
